# Page–Geilker Experiment — Conceptual Walkthrough (P/A Ontology)

The Page–Geilker experiment (1981) is one of the rare cases where the tension between quantum mechanics and gravity becomes experimentally accessible.  
Standard quantum mechanics allows macroscopic mass distributions to remain in superposition, while general relativity requires definite mass–energy to determine curvature.  
Semiclassical gravity attempts to bridge this gap by coupling the classical Einstein tensor to the expectation value of the quantum stress–energy operator.

Page and Geilker tested this hybrid theory by using a radioactive decay to choose between two macroscopic mass configurations.  
If semiclassical gravity were correct, the gravitational field should correspond to the **average** of the two mass positions.  
Instead, the torsion balance always registered a **definite** gravitational pull.

In the Possibility/Actuality (P/A) ontology, this result is expected:  
**only actualized events in A carry mass–energy, while unactualized futures in P do not gravitate.**

### Cavendish Torsion Balance

**Standard role:**  
Measures the gravitational field produced by the mass configuration.

- The measured gravitational field corresponds to the actualized mass configuration.  
- No averaging over unactualized futures occurs.

## What Semiclassical Gravity Predicts

If the decay remains in superposition, the mass should be in a superposition of left and right.  
Semiclassical gravity then predicts:

**Gᵤᵥ = 8πG ⟨ψ | Tᵤᵥ | ψ⟩**

This implies a gravitational field equal to the **weighted average** of the two mass configurations.  
The torsion balance should register a **smeared** gravitational pull.

It does not.

---

## What Page–Geilker Observed

- The gravitational field always corresponded to **one definite mass configuration**.  
- The torsion balance never responded to the expectation value.  
- This rules out semiclassical gravity and any ontology in which superposed mass is physically real.
