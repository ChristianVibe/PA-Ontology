# Mach–Zehnder Interferometer — Conceptual Walkthrough (P/A Ontology)

The Mach–Zehnder interferometer (MZI) is one of the simplest and most illuminating tools in quantum optics.  
It splits a photon into two possible paths, recombines them, and reveals how interference depends on the availability of which‑path information.  
The MZI is the conceptual foundation for more elaborate experiments such as Wheeler’s delayed choice and the delayed‑choice quantum eraser.

In the Possibility/Actuality (P/A) ontology, the MZI provides a clean demonstration of how ray bundles propagate, how apparatus define branching structures, and how actualization produces definite outcomes without collapse or paradox.

---

## Setup

A standard Mach–Zehnder interferometer consists of:

- **Beam splitter 1 (BS1)** — divides the incoming photon into two possible paths  
- **Mirrors** — redirect the two paths toward a second beam splitter  
- **Beam splitter 2 (BS2)** — recombines the paths  
- **Detectors D0 and D1** — record the photon  
- **Phase shifter** (optional) — adjusts the relative phase between the two paths

Classically, one might expect the photon to take one path or the other.  
Quantum mechanically, interference appears when the two paths remain indistinguishable.

---

## P/A Account

### Photon enters BS1

- The photon arrives with a ray bundle containing futures compatible with **transmission** and **reflection**.  
- BS1 defines a **branching structure** in P: two path futures.  
- **No actualization occurs at BS1** — the photon’s path remains open.

---

### Propagation through the arms

- The two path futures propagate independently through the interferometer.  
- If a phase shifter is present, it modifies the **compatibility structure** of the ray bundle by altering the relative phase between the two branches.  
- No actualization occurs in the arms unless a detector or interaction forces one.

---

### Recombination at BS2

- BS2 defines a new branching structure that depends on the **relative phase** between the two path futures.  
- If the paths are indistinguishable, the ray bundle contains futures that interfere:
  - **constructively** at one detector  
  - **destructively** at the other  
- If which‑path information is available, the interference structure is removed, and the ray bundle contains futures leading to both detectors with equal weight.

Still, **no actualization occurs at BS2**.  
The photon’s detection is the first actualization event.

---

### Detection at D0 or D1

- The photon interacts with a detector, defining a branching in P.  
- **Actualization selects one outcome** (Postulate 6).  
- **𝓟 prunes all incompatible futures** (Postulate 4).  
- The photon is detected at D0 or D1 with probabilities determined by the interference structure of the ray bundle.

If the paths were indistinguishable:
- the photon always appears at one detector.

If the paths were distinguishable:
- the photon appears randomly at either detector.

## Why the MZI Is Conceptually Important

The Mach–Zehnder interferometer illustrates several core principles of the P/A ontology:

- **Interference is a property of ray bundles**, not individual events.  
  A single detection never “shows interference”; only the statistical pattern does.

- **Apparatus define branching structures.**  
  Beam splitters, mirrors, and phase shifters shape the geometry of P.

- **Actualization is localized.**  
  The photon’s path is not actualized at BS1 or BS2 — only at the detector.

- **Which‑path information removes interference** by altering compatibility.  
  If the two path futures become distinguishable, 𝓟 cannot maintain the interference structure.

- **No collapse is needed.**  
  The photon’s detection is simply an actualization event, not a global wavefunction collapse.

---

## Ontological Summary

The Mach–Zehnder interferometer is the simplest laboratory in which the P/A ontology displays its core logic:

- BS1 creates multiple compatible futures.  
- The arms propagate these futures independently.  
- BS2 recombines them, producing interference if and only if the paths remain indistinguishable.  
- The detector provides the first actualization event, selecting a definite outcome and pruning incompatible futures.

No paradox, no observer dependence, and no collapse — just the geometry of possibilities shaped by the apparatus and resolved by actualization.
