# Bell’s Test — Conceptual Walkthrough (P/A Ontology)

## Introduction — From EPR to Bell: A Beam Splitter for Correlation

In 1935, Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen argued that quantum mechanics must be incomplete.  
If the theory were correct, then distant particles could display correlations that seemed to require instantaneous influence — “spooky action at a distance.”  
They proposed that hidden variables, carried locally by each particle, must determine outcomes in advance.

In 1964, John Bell formalized this tension. He derived an inequality that any **local hidden‑variable** theory must satisfy.  
If particles carry preexisting properties and no faster‑than‑light influence exists, then the correlations between distant measurements must be bounded.

Quantum mechanics predicts — and experiments confirm — that this bound is violated.  
Aspect’s photon experiments in the 1980s, and countless replications since, show correlations stronger than any local hidden‑variable model allows.

In the **Possibility/Actuality (P/A) ontology**, this violation does not arise from nonlocal collapse or superluminal influence.  
Instead, it arises from **Postulate 4**:

> Any relational property fixed in an actualization event can never be violated by any future compatible evolution.

When two particles are created in a single event, the event records relational facts — such as total spin zero — that must hold for all future possibilities.  
As each particle interacts with its local apparatus, **𝓟 prunes any futures (including futures of the distant particle) that would violate those fixed relational facts**.

Bell violation emerges from this **nonlocal pruning of possibilities in P**, not from any influence transmitted in A.

## Joint Emission in P/A

A single actualization event at the source produces two particles.

- The event records relational properties (e.g., total spin = 0).  
- By Postulate 4, these relational facts can never be violated.  
- 𝓟 removes any future in P that would contradict them.  
- Two independent ray bundles are spawned in P, one for each particle.  
- The bundles are not connected objects, but they are **jointly constrained**: any future violating the emission‑recorded relational facts is incompatible and will be pruned.

The particles themselves are fully actualized only at emission; all modal openness lies in their respective ray bundles.

This is universal: **every event entangles its participants by fixing relational facts that 𝓟 must preserve forever.**

## Measurement Sequence in P/A

### First Measurement (axis *a*)

The first particle encounters a Stern–Gerlach magnet aligned along axis *a*.

- The magnet defines two possible futures (upₐ and downₐ).  
- Actualization at the detector selects one position, determining the spin outcome along *a*.  
- A new ray bundle is spawned at that event, aligned with the actualized spin orientation.  
- 𝓟 immediately prunes all futures of the second particle that would violate the relational facts fixed at emission.

This pruning is **nonlocal in P** but involves **no influence in A**.

---

### Second Measurement (axis *a′*)

The second particle encounters its own Stern–Gerlach magnet, aligned along axis *a′*.

Its ray bundle has already had incompatible futures removed by 𝓟 to maintain the relational facts fixed at emission.

Crucially, the second particle’s ray bundle is now aligned with axis *a*, because all futures inconsistent with the first outcome have been pruned.

---

### Case 1: *a′ = a* (aligned)

- Only one future remains compatible with conservation.  
- The magnet produces a single viable lobe.  
- Actualization yields the **opposite result with certainty**.

**Outcome:** perfect anticorrelation.

---

### Case 2: *a′ ≠ a* (misaligned)

- The second particle’s ray bundle is aligned with axis *a*.  
- When this bundle enters a Stern–Gerlach magnet oriented along *a′*, it is partially compatible with each *a′* outcome.  
- Relative compatibility is determined by the angle between *a* and *a′*.  
- The geometry of the *a′* magnet reshapes the bundle into two lobes whose relative weights follow the cosine law.  
- Actualization selects one position, determining the spin along *a′*.

**Outcome:** probabilities match the quantum prediction.

This is exactly the same mechanism as the z → x case in the Stern–Gerlach walkthrough.

## Ontological Summary

- Every actualization event fixes relational properties that can never be violated (Postulate 4).  
- The joint emission of two particles imposes a permanent conservation constraint.  
- Each measurement defines a branching structure in P; actualization selects one branch.  
- 𝓟 enforces the emission‑recorded relational facts by pruning incompatible futures — including futures of the distant particle.  
- This pruning is nonlocal in P but involves no influence in A.

The correlation function emerges from the geometry of the remaining compatible futures:

**E(a, b) = −cos(θₐᵦ)**

For the standard Bell settings (a, a′, b, b′), P/A yields:

**S ≈ 2.8**

This violates Bell’s inequality without invoking:

- nonlocal collapse

The correlation arises from **conservation‑driven pruning by 𝓟**, not from any dynamical influence between the particles.
