# Stern–Gerlach Experiment — Conceptual Walkthrough (P/A Ontology)

## Introduction — A Beam Splitter for Ontology

In 1922, Otto Stern and Walther Gerlach sent a beam of silver atoms through a non‑uniform magnetic field and onto a detection plate.  
Classical physics predicted a continuous smear of impacts, reflecting random orientations of atomic magnetic moments.  
Instead, the beam split cleanly into two discrete spots — revealing that the component of spin along the field axis is quantized.

In standard quantum mechanics, this is explained by treating spin as an intrinsic two‑valued property, with measurement “collapsing” the spin state into either up or down along the chosen axis.

In the **Possibility/Actuality (P/A) ontology**, the empirical facts remain the same, but the explanatory grammar is different:

- no wavefunction  
- no collapse  
- no observer‑dependent state reduction  

A particle is always fully actualized at each event; it never carries partial commitments.  
What evolves between events is not the particle but its **ray bundle in P** — a structured set of possible future trajectories and spin orientations shaped entirely by the apparatus.

The Stern–Gerlach magnet becomes a clean demonstration of how the apparatus defines the branching structure of P, and how actualization selects one branch without altering the particle’s internal state.

## Experimental Setup in P/A Terms

### Emitter

**Standard role:**  
A hot oven emits neutral silver atoms with random spin orientations.

**P/A role:**  
- Emission is an **actualization event** in A.  
- A ray bundle is spawned in P, encoding possible trajectories and possible spin orientations.  
- Before any measurement, the bundle is compatible with both “up” and “down” outcomes along any axis.  
- The particle itself is fully actualized; the openness lies in the ray bundle.

---

### Collimator

**Standard role:**  
Narrows the beam to a well‑defined path.

**P/A role:**  
- The collimator **prunes rays** incompatible with the narrow beam geometry.  
- It shapes the spatial structure of the ray bundle without actualizing anything.  
- Spin remains fully open in P.

---

### Stern–Gerlach Magnet

**Standard role:**  
Applies a spatially varying magnetic field that deflects atoms according to their spin projection along the magnet’s axis.

**P/A role:**  
- The magnetic field couples spin to spatial deflection.  
- Rays in the bundle separate into two compatibility lobes:
  - one corresponding to “spin‑up”  
  - one corresponding to “spin‑down”  
- The splitting is entirely due to apparatus geometry.  
- No actualization occurs inside the magnet.  
- The magnet defines **two candidate outcomes** that may later be actualized.

---

### Detector Screen

**Standard role:**  
Records the arrival position of each atom.

**P/A role:**  
- Rays reaching the screen are pruned and added to the set of candidate detection outcomes.  
- Local compatibility weighting determines their relative likelihood.  
- **Actualization selects one ray**, producing a single detection event in A.  
- The vertical displacement of the event encodes the committed spin value.

## Case 1 — First Measurement Along the z‑Axis

### Emission
- A particle is actualized at the emitter.  
- A ray bundle is spawned with spin‑compatible rays pointing in all directions.  
- Along the z‑axis, the bundle is compatible with both “up” and “down.”

### Passage through the magnet
- The magnetic field separates the ray bundle into two spatial lobes.  
- These lobes represent the two possible spin‑z outcomes.  
- The particle is not “in a superposition”; the openness lies in P.

### Detection
- Rays from each lobe reach different regions of the screen.  
- Actualization selects one ray, fixing a definite position.  
- Because position is correlated with spin orientation, this determines the spin‑z value.  
- Over many trials, two discrete spots appear — the signature of spin quantization.

## Case 2 — Sequential Stern–Gerlach Measurements

### z → z
- First magnet: actualization fixes either z‑up or z‑down.  
- A new ray bundle is spawned, compatible with only one z‑orientation.  
- Second magnet (same axis): only one lobe is compatible.  
- **Outcome:** same value with certainty.

---

### z → x
- First magnet: actualization fixes z‑up or z‑down.  
- A new ray bundle is spawned, aligned with the actualized z‑orientation.  
- Second magnet (x‑axis): the z‑aligned bundle is equally compatible with x‑up and x‑down.  
- The x‑magnet’s geometry creates two equally weighted lobes.  
- Actualization selects one.  
- **Outcome:** 50/50 distribution along x.

---

### z → x → z
- First magnet (z): actualization fixes z‑up or z‑down.  
- Second magnet (x): two equally weighted x‑lobes; actualization selects one.  
- A new ray bundle is spawned, aligned with the actualized x‑orientation.  
- Third magnet (z): the x‑aligned bundle is equally compatible with z‑up and z‑down.  
- Actualization selects one.  
- **Outcome:** 50/50 distribution along z — the apparatus reopens both z‑possibilities.

---

## Ontological Summary

- A Stern–Gerlach magnet **does not cause actualization**; it reshapes the ray bundle in P.  
- Actualization occurs only at the detector.  
- The particle is fully actualized at each event and never carries partial commitments.  
- All stochasticity arises from **apparatus‑defined geometry**, not from the particle.  
- Repeated measurements along the same axis yield deterministic outcomes.  
- Measurements along different axes reopen compatibility, restoring probabilistic outcomes.
