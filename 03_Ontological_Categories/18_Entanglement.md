# Entanglement

Two carriers (in particular, particles) $p$ and $p'$ are said to be
entangled if they share the same most recent actualized event. This
defines an equivalence relation

$$
p \sim p'.
$$

The shared event establishes joint constraints—via conserved quantities
encoded in $C$—on the admissible futures of the involved carriers. As a
result, their ray bundles cannot be updated independently.

If $p \sim p'$, then pruning must act coherently across their ray
bundles. In particular, a ray $r_{p'} \in R_{p'}$ is removed if it is
incompatible with every ray in $R_p$ that survives the pruning induced by
the shared event. Equivalently,

$$
P(R_{p'}, e) = R'_{p'},
$$

where $R'_{p'}$ contains only those rays admitting at least one waypoint
compatible with a surviving waypoint on some ray of $p$.

Whenever a carrier participates in a new actualized event, its previous
entanglement relations are replaced by those induced by the new event.

---

## Structural Role

Entanglement enforces joint modal constraints among carriers that share
an actualized event. It ensures:

- coherent pruning across their ray bundles,
- conservation‑law consistency in admissible futures,
- and coupled modal evolution for carriers linked by shared events.

Entanglement is the mechanism by which multi‑carrier interactions impose
shared constraints on future modal trajectories.

---

## Relations

- Entanglement is defined by shared most recent events in actuality $A$.
- Entangled carriers must undergo pruning coherently across their ray
  bundles.
- Compatibility between rays of entangled carriers determines which
  futures survive.
- New actualized events overwrite previous entanglement relations.
- Entanglement couples possibility‑space evolution across multiple
  carriers.
